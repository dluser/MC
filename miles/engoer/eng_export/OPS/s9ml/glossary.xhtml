Glossary

Build

Build

To compile and package the content in a Habitat project to create an Inkling title. The Inkling title can then be downloaded to and viewed in the Inkling app on supported devices.

Caption

Caption

Text that describes an image. It appears outside the image, usually below it.

Citation

Citation

The information that documents the source from which an author took content. Citations that are in a print book’s footnotes and endnotes are stored in Remarks.

Enhancement

Enhancement

Interactive elements within an Inkling title. The majority enhancements are created using widgets, but in its broadest use, the term can also refer to poptips, inline audio, and other improvements made possible by the Inkling platform.

Inline image

Inline image

An image that appears on a line of text. Special symbols or equations in the text that cannot be rendered in HTML are rendered as inline images.

Pattern

Pattern

Inkling term for a snippet of HTML code that includes a reference to CSS. Readymade patterns are available in Habitat's Pattern Picker and Inkling’s Pattern Library.

Revision

Revision

Every time a project team member saves a change to content in Habitat, that revision of the file is archived with a unique number. A revision automatically generates analyses of the content, and the results are presented in the Reports area of Habitat. Because every revision is given a unique number and is archived, you can view the state of a project at any moment in its history.

Sandbox chapter

Sandbox chapter

A chapter that is part of a Habitat project but is not meant to be part of your final Inkling title. It can contain examples of content, reusable HTML patterns, or exploratory material. When you create a such chapter in Table of Contents, identify it as a sandbox chapter in the Inspector.

Ticket

Ticket

Tickets are comments or instructions about content. Create tickets in Habitat Proof mode. Use them to identify errors in content, to suggest improvements, and so on. Assign a ticket to yourself or another team member who can respond to your comment or fix an error.

Widget

Widget

Widgets enable you to put javascript within <object> tags to provide interactive content in Inkling pages.

XHTML

XHTML

XHTML is a stricter version of HTML. For example, in XHTML all tags must be closed, so use <br /> rather than <br> and <img /> rather than <img>. Habitat's well-formed HTML is a subset of the XHTML standards; Habitat requires code that validates to XHTML standards to ensure that content displays correctly on all supported devices. See Inkling standards for HTML and alsoXHTML Reference.

Analysis

Analysis

Separating complex ideas, materials, texts and studying their different parts in order to discover how the parts relate to one another         

Attitude

Attitude

In a rhetorical situation, the orientation of the voice toward the message it wants to convey.         

Audience

Audience

The individual or group whom the writer intends to address. The audience is the intended readers or listeners of a text.         

Claims

Claims

The details, facts, and explanations that help clarify the main point of an essay         

Concept

Concept

In advertising, the theme of a brand, which is relayed through the message, the product, the color choices, the layout design, and the graphic elements         

Concision

Concision

The art of using the fewest words possible to convey an idea.         

Conclusion

Conclusion

The final paragraph in an essay, which should not introduce new claims, but should instead wrap up the paper, explain how the paper proves the thesis, or explain the paper opens up additional thinking about your subject.         

Content

Content

Refers to all the written substance in a document.          

Diction

Diction

Word choice         

Author

Author

The originator or creator of a work, especially of a literary composition.

Source

Source

The person, place or thing from which something (information, goods, etc. ) comes or is acquired.

Expository Writing

Expository Writing

Derived from the word "expose," expository writing seeks to expose, explain, describe, define, or inform.

Thesis

Thesis

A claim or theory that must be supported with evidence to argue for or against a specific idea or position.

Keyword

Keyword

A word which occurs in a text more often than we would expect to occur by chance alone.

Summary

Summary

A condensed version of the background or main ideas of a text.

Synthesis

Synthesis

The formation of something complex or coherent by combining simpler things.

Evaluation

Evaluation

An assessment or judgment based on specific criteria.

Objectivity

Objectivity

The ability to perceive a subject without being influenced by personal biases or emotions.

Syntax

Syntax

The way in which words and phrases are crafted and combined in sentences.

Perspective

Perspective

In analysis, your personal insight into a text; what a text means to you and why you think it's significant.

Argument

Argument

The effort to use rhetorical appeals to influence an audience and achieve a certain set of purposes and outcomes; argumentative writing must take a stance.

Rhetorical situation

Rhetorical situation

Circumstances under which you communicate, which includes your purpose, audience, tone, genre, and medium of communication (online, face-to-face, text message, formal paper, etc.)

Introduction

Introduction

Frames a writer's paper by introducing the issue at hand, establishes the writer's position, and identifies the writer's scope of coverage.

Tone

Tone

The attitude the writer conveys about the subject. Also, the connection established between the voice and the audience.

Thesis Statement

Thesis Statement

The main point of a paper; the idea of hypothesis a writer is trying to prove.

Comprehension

Comprehension

Discusses elements that are similar

Supporting points

Supporting points

The details, facts, and explanations that develop and clarify the main point.

Report

Report

A document that records and conveys information to readers in a clear, concise, and visually appealing manner.

Persuasive writing

Persuasive writing

Writing created to convice, motivate, or move readers toward a certain point of view or opinion.

Prewriting

Prewriting

The first stage of the writing process, which may consist of a combination of outlining, diagramming, storyboarding, and clustering.

Chronological order

Chronological order

Presenting an idea or story in the order in which the events happened, from first to last event.

Academia

Academia

The environment or community concerned with the pursuit of research, education, and scholarship.

Academic writing

Academic writing

Refers to a particular style of expression that researchers use to define the intellectual boundaries of their disciplines and their areas of expertise.

Analytical report

Analytical report

Presents information with a comprehensive analysis to solve problems, demonstrate relationships, or make recommendations.

Assertation

Assertation

Statement that presents a points of view.

Brainstorm

Brainstorm

A spontaneous group discussion to produce ideas and ways of solving problems.

Characters

Characters

The people who inhabit the story and move it forward.

Glossary term

Glossary term

An alphabetical list of terms or words found in or relating to a specific subject, text, or dialect, with explanations; a brief dictionary.

Closing paragraph

Closing paragraph

The final paragraph in an essay, which should not introduce new claims, but should instead wrap up the paper, explain how the paper proves the thesis, or explain the paper opens up additional thinking about your subject.

Comparison

Comparison

Discusses elements that are similar.

Conflict

Conflict

The primary problem or obstacle that unfolds in the plot that the protagonist must solve or overcome by the end of the narrative.

Context

Context

The circumstances that form the setting for an event, statement, or idea, and in terms of which it can be fully understood and assessed.

Contrast

Contrast

Discusses elements that are different.

Controlling idea

Controlling idea

The main idea upon which a writer builds their thesis statement.

Deductive Reasoning

Deductive Reasoning

Beginning an argument with a general principle, which is referred to as a major premise, then a related premise is applied to the major premise and a conclusion is formed.

Editing

Editing

A step in the revision process that includes fixing typos and grammatical errors.

Emotional truth

Emotional truth

A visceral, heartfelt connection that arises between reader and character or characters through the unfolding (and possibly the resolution) of an invented, narrated conflict, a connection so powerful that the reader perceives reality and truth in what is known to be pretend, known to be fiction.

Outline

Outline

A detailed summary of the important points in a text.

Research

Research

Diligent inquiry or examination to seek or revise facts, principles, theories, applications, et cetera; laborious or continued search after truth.

Counterargument

Counterargument

An argument that is opposed to another argument.

Topic sentences

Topic sentences

A statement of the main idea of the paragraph in which it occurs.

Quotation

Quotation

A group of words, usually three or more, that are borrowed from another writer or speaker. The use of quotation marks signals , to the reader, that this text is not the writer's original thought.

Passive voice

Passive voice

A sentence construction where the subject is not clear or the subject is not performing the action in the sentence. 

Rhetorical questions

Rhetorical questions

A rhetorical question is a question that you ask without expecting an answer. The question might be one that does not have an answer. It might also be one that has an obvious answer but you have asked the question to make a point, to persuade or for literary effect.

Thesaurus

Thesaurus

A publication, usually in the form of a book, that provides synonyms (and sometimes antonyms) for the words of a given language.

Synonyms

Synonyms

A word or phrase with a meaning that is the same as, or very similar to, another word or phrase.

Misinterpreatation

Misinterpreatation

to interpret, explain, or understand incorrectly.

Reiterate

Reiterate

Say something again or a number of times, typically for emphasis or clarity.

Personal narrative

Personal narrative

A personal narrative tells the true story of something that happened to the writer.

Ethos

Ethos

Appeals to the credibilty, reputation, and trustworthiness of the speaker or writer (most closely associated with the voice).

Evidence

Evidence

It is factual information that helps the reader reach a conclusion and form an opinion about something. Evidence is given in research work or is quoted in essays and thesis statements but is paraphrased by the writer. If it is given as it is, then it is quoted properly within quotation marks.

Explanations

Explanations

Statements that reveal how the examples support and/or complicate a writer's statements.

Facts

Facts

Evidence that cannot be disputed.

Fallacies

Fallacies

Errors in reasoning. 

Flash(ing) back

Flash(ing) back

Used in a narrative to introduce a prior, related event in the story.

Freewriting

Freewriting

Writing that comes out as a stream of thoughts unencumbered by grammar, spelling or a few of where it is heading.

Genre

Genre

A category of artistic composition, as in music or literature, characterized by similarities in form, style, or subject matter.

Heuristics

Heuristics

Discovery procedures that help a writer explore their ideas; for example, the Pentad.

Imagery

Imagery

A description that appeals to one of the five senses.

Inductive Reasoning

Inductive Reasoning

The consideration of a number of results and forming a generalization based on those results.

Informational report

Informational report

Informs or instructs and presents details of events, activities, individuals, or conditions without analysis.

Issue dialogue

Issue dialogue

Creation dialogue about a topic during the early analysis stage that focuses on the most extreme positions then moving towards more reasonable compromises. 

Judgments

Judgments

Conclusions drawn from the given facts; they are more credibile than opitions.

Leads

Leads

In an academic essay, an opening, usually located in the introduction, that hooks the reader into wanting to read further.

Loaded terms

Loaded terms

Words infused with negative associations.

Logos

Logos

Appeals to reason, logic, and facts in an argument. In a rhetorical situation, it is the appeal most closely associated with the message.

Message

Message

In a rhetorical situation, a message is the text being conveyed by the speaker.

Mood

Mood

The emotional weight or atmosphere of a story, created through details, description and other craft features.

Pathos

Pathos

Appeals to the emotions and cultural beliefs of the listeners or readers. In a rhetorical situation, it is the appeal most closely associated with the audience.

Pentad

Pentad

A learning tool (developed by social philosopher Kenneth Burke) that helps break apart information into five interrelated components that determine its overall shape and direction (Act, Agent, Agency, Scene, Purpose)

Personal obersvation

Personal obersvation

Similar to testimony, but consists of ones own testimony; it reflects what you know to be true because you have experiences and have formed either opinions or judgements.

Persona

Persona

Comes from the Latin word for mask; it's the version of the writer that they want to illustrate for the reader in a memoir.

Plot

Plot

The events as they unfold in sequence.

Precise diction

Precise diction

Words that are clear and specific.

Present tense

Present tense

A tense expressing an action that is currently going on or habitually performed, or a state that currently or generally exists.

Primary support

Primary support

The major points a writer chooses to expand on in their thesis. It is the most important information a writer uses to argue their point of view.

Proofreading

Proofreading

Reviewing a paper for general consistency and clarity.

Purpose

Purpose

The reason the writer composes the writing.

Qualitative visuals

Qualitative visuals

Present images that appea tot he audience's emotions.

Quantitative visuals

Quantitative visuals

Present data graphically to logically appeal to the audience.

Reception

Reception

The manner in which the audience receives the message conveyed.

Reflection

Reflection

The sense and interpretation that a writer makes of the events that transpire in a memoir.

Reverse outline

Reverse outline

An outline that is created after the initial prewriting process.

Rhetorical fallacies

Rhetorical fallacies

Fallacies are reasoning errors, which abuse the power of logical, emotional, or ehtical appeals.

Rhetorical mode

Rhetorical mode

Rhetorical modes (also known as modes of discourse) describe the variety, conventions, and purposes of the major kinds of language-based communication, particularly writing and speaking. Four of the most common rhetorical modes and their purpose are narration, description, exposition, and argumentation.

Rhetorical vecotrs

Rhetorical vecotrs

Diagonal lines throughout an image that create action.

Rogerian argument

Rogerian argument

Named for its creator, Carl Rogers, a type of argument that aims for true compromise between two positions.

Scene

Scene

The place where an incident in real life or fiction occurs or occurred.

Semiotics

Semiotics

The study of signs and symbols and their use or interpretation.

Sentence variety

Sentence variety

The creation of sentences of various lengths and types.

Setting

Setting

Where a story takes place.

Signal phrase

Signal phrase

An introduction that introduces material that summarizes someone else's work or reports someone else's findings. 

Significance

Significance

Statements that reveal the importance of the analysis to the writer's personal and/or cultural concerns.

Stock phrases

Stock phrases

Sayings repeated by a person or group that then becomes associated with that group. Stock phrases can be problematic in academic writing when they are used without fully considering their implications. Also, like cliches, stock phrases can lose their significance when they are re-used often.

Style

Style

The literary element that describes the ways that the author uses words — the author's word choice, sentence structure, figurative language, and sentence arrangement all work together to establish mood, images, and meaning in the text.

Testimony

Testimony

Direct quotations from either an eyewitness or an expert witness.

Theme

Theme

The ultimate message the narrative is trying to express; it can either be explicit or implicit.

Toulminian argument

Toulminian argument

Named for its creator, Stephen Toulminian, includes three components: a claim, stated grounds to support the claim, and ustated assuptions called warrents.

Transitions

Transitions

A word, phrase, sentence, or paragraph that clarifies how the writer's ideas are connected.

Tropes

Tropes

Words or phrases that point toward a figurative meaning.

Visual literacy

Visual literacy

The ability to read multimodal text.

Visual rhetoric

Visual rhetoric

Representations and images designed to convince people instead, or in addition to, using words; the study of what impression visuals give a viewer.

Voice

Voice

Refers to elements of the author's tone, phrasing, and style that are recognizable unique to her or him.

Working thesis statement

Working thesis statement

An indefinite statement that a writer makes about their topic early in the writing process for the purpose of planning or guiding their writing.

Worthwhile assertation

Worthwhile assertation

Reveals a perspective on your subject that provides possibilities for further exploration.

Metaphor

Metaphor

A thing regarded as representative or symbolic of something else, especially something abstract.

Database

Database

A collection of (usually) organized information in a regular structure, usually but not necessarily in a machine-readable format accessible by a computer.

Data

Data

Pieces of information.

Abstract

Abstract

An abstract is a brief summary of a research article that is used to help the reader quickly ascertain the paper's purpose.

Research Paper

Research Paper

A substantial piece of academic writing, usually done as a requirement for a class, in which the author independently researches a topic and writes a description of the research findings.

Primary sources

Primary sources

Firsthand sources of information. 

Secondary sources

Secondary sources

Discuss, interpret, analyze, consolidate, or otherwise rework information from primary sources.

Hanging Indent

Hanging Indent

A formatting style for citations in both MLA and APA reference pages in which   every line except the first is a half-inch away from the left margin.

et al.

et al.

“et al.” is short for “et alia,” which means “and other people” in Latin. In your paper, if you write Pauling et al. reported X findings, you are saying, Pauling and others reported X findings.

aphorism

aphorism

A short phrase that contains a general truth.

Simile

Simile

A figure of speech which uses the words like or as to create a comparison between objects, people and/or concepts. Example--You are bright as the sun.

Critical Thinking

Critical Thinking

The act of  being openly engaged with a text while considering its multiple possibilities.  

Principles of Citing

Principles of Citing

In MLA format, the hierarchical rules for creating works cited entries.

Style

Style

The way writing is created to respond to the audience, context, and purpose. A writers' style can be created by the use of their individual word choice, voice, and sentence construction.

They/Their

They/Their

The use of they instead of he or she was a conscious choice on the part of the instructors. For non-gender conforming people, the use of they allows for a third person pronoun usage. Some teachers may not be on board with this grammatical choice, even though grammar and language are always evolving. The pronoun you decide to use is up to you. For further proof and evidence that they is an acceptable word choice, you can refer to Merriam-Webster Dictionary, which is on board with "they" as a singular pronoun--(https://www.merriam-webster.com/.../singular-nonbinary-they), plus the Washington Post adopted it in their style guide, and the American dialect association declared it the word of the year.
